Introduction/Background

The US presidential election is incredibly important since our country is arguably the most influential in the world. It not only directly influences our domestic economic market and over 300 million citizens, but also the global market and consequently the entire world. Understanding complex factors that affect a presidential election are extremely important, particularly this year as it is an election year.

An interesting thing to note is that few studies look at how a variety of features influence the election on a state level. We plan to investigate features such as demographics, government data, and economic data to see what features help to predict the outcome of an election. Additionally, the results for most studies are binary, whether one candidate will win the election. We plan to look at the ratio between votes for the democratic party versus votes for the republican party.

Methods

Current forecasting methods typically used are financial prediction markets and pre-polling which have shown to be quite accurate, one article citing it at 1.5% and 2.1% error respectively days before the 2008 election. However, the limitations of this are the national popular vote in U.S. presidential elections tend to oscillate wildly in the months before the election takes place, so we intend to not use polling and prediction market methods in favor of more objective data points. We believe this combined with machine learning techniques will allow us to find more complex trends that would be hard to notice via more classical methods. Around halfway through our project duration, we want to gather all data sets we would need from at least 2 election cycles, a general feature list with relevant feature engineering, and a set of most impactful features found via preliminary results from unsupervised learning.

Data Combination/Cleaning

When collecting our data, we wanted to consider features from reliable data sources that were most likely to have an effect on political data. We focused on three different categories of data: demographic, economic, and political data. The demographic data, extracted from the census, highlights sex, race, age (in ranges), and hispanic descent. Economic data, including GDP, personal consumption expenditure, and personal income are sourced from the bureau of economic analysis. Lastly, presidential approval rating from Gallup polls and data on congressional seats completed the last component of our feature set: political data.

Due to constraints from being able to meet in person, we decided that entering all of our data into a Google Sheet would be the best way to combine all of our features. We had 100 rows, one for each state in the years 2012 and 2016, and 26 columns. Many of our values within features were very large, so we normalized the features between 0 and 1. The Google Sheet was then exported as a csv file so that we were able to use machine learning libraries and parse data using Python with ease.

Unsupervised Learning Portion:

Principal Component Analysis

When we created the correlation matrix of our dataset, we saw just how many of our features were strongly correlated with others. Because of this, we realized we could distill our dataset down to a small number of features and still be able to create accurate clusters, perhaps even more accurate clusters than what could be made with the original dataset. To do this, we relied on the PCA algorithm. The PCA algorithm takes in a dataset and creates a new set of orthogonal features, each a linear transformation of the original features, and orders the features by the variance they contribute to the total variance of the dataset. We used the PCA class of the scikit-learn python library to run PCA on our dataset, and then plotted the explained variance ratio of each new feature.

As you can see in the graphs, the vast majority of the variance in the dataset is contained by only a handful of the variables. We decided to limit our dataset to the top 5 generated features, as they contained nearly 95% of the variance of the dataset.

We compared our KMeans results with 2 clusters to the true results of the 2012 and 2016 election in order to calculate a misclassification rate.

Supervised Learning Portion: With regards to next steps, we plan to begin work on the supervised learning portion of the project. We plan to use supervised learning to train a model which will be able to predict how a state will lean in an election, given the relevant features. The algorithms we plan to use to analyze the data are support vector machines and neural networks. Support vector machines will help us classify the states into their corresponding political groups. With creating a neural network, we will need to do a bit more work. We’ll need to include more data points (election data from 2004 and 2008) in addition to the current data points we have for 2012 and 2016. We plan to double the data points so that we have more information with which to train our model. For these algorithms, we plan to use the five components which resulted from our principal component analysis from the unsupervised learning portion of the project. Once we train our models, we plan to test them with data pulled from the recent general election (2020). However, since the relevant features will most likely not have the proper data yet, we will need to use data from the previous year (2019).

Results

The specific problem that we are going to solve is predicting the ratio of Republican to Democratic popular vote by state. Our approach is unique as we intend to use machine learning algorithms, not regression or other traditionally linear tactics and data that is demographic, economic, or descriptive of state politics. We anticipate success based on two factors: past works and our intention to increase the level of granularity within our features. Past projects that focus on predicting political outcomes such as “Fundamental Models for Forecasting Elections” by Hummel and Rothschild and “Predicting U.S. president election result based on Google Insights” by Stanford students utilize features that are common to those that we want to incorporate and validate our belief that predictions can be made accurately on a state level.

Discussion

One minor risk is we believe that election predictions can affect voting behavior, but overall we do not think that there are significant risks associated with this project. In the final stages of our model, we will run a 5-fold cross validation with our existing data. We will then use our model to predict the 2020 presidential election, which will allow us to verify the model.

Our project has two main important contributions. By using unsupervised learning, we will be able to determine which features have either a direct or indirect influence on each state's vote. This way, future polls could implement this information in order to be more accurate. By using supervised learning, we will hopefully build a more accurate model which would be used to predict future elections.
